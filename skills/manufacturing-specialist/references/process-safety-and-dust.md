# Process safety, major hazard facilities & combustible dust — good practice (NZ/AU)

Flagship reference for `manufacturing-specialist`. These are the **low-frequency / catastrophic-
consequence** risks that a routine risk assessment can miss — a **loss of containment** at a major
hazard facility, or a **dust explosion** in a food/wood/metal plant — that can kill many people at once,
including the public. Grounded in the **NZ HSW (Major Hazard Facilities) Regulations 2016** and the **AU
WHS major-hazard-facility regime + hazardous-chemicals Code**. Standards are **verify-pointers**.

> **Not legal advice; not process-safety engineering.** Major-hazard and dust-explosion controls require
> **competent process-safety / fire-and-explosion engineering** and (for an MHF) a formal safety case.

---

## 1. Process safety vs personal safety

**Personal (occupational) safety** prevents the everyday injuries (slips, manual handling, machine
contact). **Process safety** prevents the **low-frequency, high-consequence** events — fires,
explosions, toxic releases — from a **loss of containment** of energy or hazardous material. They need
**different controls**: a good lost-time-injury record says nothing about whether the plant is about to
blow up. Manage process safety explicitly, through the integrity of the **plant, barriers and
management systems**.

---

## 2. Major Hazard Facilities (MHF)

- An **MHF** is a site that **stores, handles or processes hazardous chemicals above threshold
  quantities** (oil/gas, chemical plants, large fuel/chemical/dangerous-goods stores). Key risks:
  **fire, explosion, toxic release** affecting workers **and the surrounding community**.
- It is a **licensed, safety-case regime**:
  - **NZ:** **HSW (Major Hazard Facilities) Regulations 2016** (WorkSafe) — lower/upper-tier duties, a
    **safety case** demonstrating the major-incident risks are managed, and notification/licensing.
  - **AU:** the **WHS Regulations (major hazard facilities)** — chemicals in **Schedule 15** above
    threshold; determination, **operator licence** (apply within the set period), a **safety
    assessment** and safety-case obligations; the operator holds the licence and the duties.
- Core obligations: **identify all major-incident scenarios**, demonstrate controls reduce risk **SFAIRP**,
  maintain the **integrity of safety-critical barriers**, plan for **emergencies** with the community/
  responders, and consult workers.
- **If a site is near a threshold, get specialist advice** — determination can be triggered by the
  regulator.

## 3. Combustible dust (the under-recognised explosion risk)

Many ordinary manufacturing dusts explode. The **dust-explosion pentagon** — all five present at once:

1. **Combustible dust** (fuel) — flour, sugar, starch, milk powder, **wood**, **metals** (aluminium,
   magnesium), plastics, coal.
2. **Oxygen** (air).
3. **Ignition source** — spark, hot surface, friction, static, ember.
4. **Dispersion** — dust suspended in a cloud.
5. **Confinement** — in a vessel, duct, room or silo (raises the pressure).

Remove any one and you prevent the explosion. **Secondary explosions** — a small blast lofts settled
dust which then ignites — are what kill, so **accumulated dust is the hazard**.

**Controls:**
- **Housekeeping first** — prevent dust accumulation (no layers on beams, ducts, surfaces); safe cleaning
  (no dry sweeping/compressed air that creates clouds; use rated vacuum systems).
- **Hazardous-area classification** — classify areas where combustible dust clouds may occur and **control
  ignition sources** (rated electrical equipment, bonding/earthing for static, no hot work without
  permit, friction/hot-bearing control).
- **Engineering** — dust extraction/collection (designed for dust-explosion risk), **explosion
  venting/suppression/isolation** on equipment (mills, dryers, dust collectors, conveyors), inerting
  where needed.
- **Detection & management** — spark detection on ducting, maintenance integrity, and a dust-explosion
  hazard assessment by a competent person.

## 4. Process-safety management (the system that holds it together)

- **Know your hazards** — process hazard analysis (HAZOP/what-if), inventory and conditions.
- **Engineered barriers** — and a program to keep **safety-critical equipment** tested and maintained
  (mechanical integrity); manage **change** (no undocumented modifications) and pre-start safety reviews.
- **Operating discipline** — procedures, competency, permits (hot work, line-breaking), alarm management.
- **Emergency planning** — with workers, neighbours and emergency services; fire/explosion/toxic-release
  scenarios.
- **Learn** — investigate process-safety near-misses (loss of containment, overpressure) as seriously as
  injuries.

---

## 5. Critical questions to consider

- Is the site an **MHF** (or near a threshold) — is there a **safety case / safety assessment** and
  licence, and are **safety-critical barriers** maintained?
- Are **combustible-dust** hazards identified — is **dust accumulation prevented**, are **ignition sources
  controlled** in classified areas, and is equipment fitted with **explosion protection**?
- Is **process safety managed distinctly from personal safety** (integrity, change, permits, emergency)?
- Is there **competent process-safety/fire-and-explosion engineering** behind the controls?
- Is the **emergency plan** real and shared with responders and the community?
