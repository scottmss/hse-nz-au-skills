# Overlapping duties (model WHS Act s 46)

When more than one duty holder has a WHS duty for the same matter, each must work with the others.
Based on the Safe Work Australia model WHS Act and the model Code of Practice *Work health and safety
consultation, cooperation and coordination*. **Not legal advice**; s 46 is a model-law verify-pointer
— confirm the enacted number (and note **Victoria's OHS Act 2004 differs**).

## The duty — consult, cooperate, coordinate

**Model WHS Act s 46:** if more than one person has a duty in relation to the **same matter**, each
must, **so far as is reasonably practicable, consult, cooperate with, and coordinate** activities with
all others who have a duty in relation to that same matter.

- This is the Australian analogue of NZ HSWA **s 34** — the mechanics are materially the same.
- It exists to **close gaps and avoid duplication** where duties overlap.
- A duty cannot be transferred or contracted away; failing s 46 is an offence (penalties vary by
  jurisdiction — verify).

## When do duties overlap?

A shared workplace is **not** required. Duties overlap:

- in a **shared workplace** — more than one PCBU influences or controls the work on a site (e.g. a
  shopping centre, port, construction site); and
- in a **contracting chain** — head contractor, contractors and subcontractors (or a client and its
  contractors) who may not share a site.

## Who is best placed to control the risk? (influence and control)

Responsibility is apportioned by each PCBU's **capacity to influence and control** the matter:

- **the more influence and control** a PCBU has over a matter, **the more responsibility** it has;
- influence/control flows from control over the **work activity**, the **workplace**, and the
  **workers**;
- **a business's size or resources does not equate to control** — the largest party is not
  automatically the most responsible;
- a PCBU with less control may discharge its duty through **reasonable arrangements** with a
  more-influential PCBU — **but entering an arrangement does not transfer the duty**; each must still
  **monitor** that what was agreed actually happens, and must **not push risk down a contracting
  chain**.

## Meeting the duty in practice

The same approach as NZ — plan ahead, identify the shared risks, agree how each will be controlled and
**who is best placed** to control each, define roles/responsibilities/actions, and keep communicating
and monitoring as the work changes. The **detailed consultation question checklists** (work
activities, H&S risks, ongoing communication) are set out in the NZ reference and apply equally here:
`../../worksafe-nz-specialist/references/overlapping-duties.md`.

Regulators expect duty holders to be able to **explain the steps** taken to consult, cooperate and
coordinate, and to keep arrangements **sensible and proportionate** to their influence and control.
