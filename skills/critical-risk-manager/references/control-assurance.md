# Control assurance

Once critical controls are identified, the question that actually protects people is not "do we
have a control?" but **"is the control real, in place, effective, and verified — right now?"**
That is control assurance. A bow tie full of named controls that nobody checks gives false
comfort; assurance is how you tell a paper barrier from a real one.

## The three assurance questions

For each **critical control**, answer all three:

1. **In place** — does the control physically/organisationally exist for this work, at this site,
   today? (Not "we have a policy" — is the rated sling actually here and tagged in date?)
2. **Effective** — if it works as intended, does it actually stop the threat or limit the
   consequence? Is it the right control, correctly specified for the energy involved?
3. **Verified** — how do we *know* 1 and 2 are true? Who checks, how often, and what's the
   evidence? A control with no verification is an assumption.

A critical control that fails any of the three is a **gap** — record it as a finding, not a tick.

## Control strength (reliability)

Rank each critical control by how much it depends on human behaviour in the moment:

| Strength | Type | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Strongest | Elimination / substitution | The energy or exposure is gone. |
| Strong | Engineering / isolation (passive) | Works without anyone acting — guard, physical barrier, rated structure. |
| Moderate | Engineering (active) | Works but can fail or be defeated — interlock, alarm, LMI cut-out. |
| Weak | Administrative | Depends on people doing the right thing every time — procedure, permit, competency, exclusion by signage. |
| Weakest | PPE | Protects one person, last line, fails quietly. |

A critical risk whose only critical control is **weak or weakest** is a defensibility and safety
problem — recommend a stronger control where reasonably practicable, or strong escalation-factor
controls and verification around the weak one.

## Verification — turning controls into evidence

For each critical control, define a **verification activity**:

- **What** is checked (the control's performance standard — what "good" looks like).
- **Who** is accountable (an owner — a role, not "the team").
- **How often** (frequency proportional to how fast it can degrade).
- **Evidence** (inspection record, pre-start check, tag, permit, competency record, test result).
- **What happens on failure** (the response when verification finds the control absent/degraded).

A control with a named owner, a performance standard, a verification frequency, and an evidence
trail is *assured*. Anything less is *assumed*.

## Escalation factors and assurance

Each escalation factor (fatigue, competency lapse, bypassed interlock, overdue inspection,
complacency, weather) degrades a control's assurance. For critical controls, the escalation-factor
controls should themselves be verified — otherwise the assurance is only as good as the weakest
unchecked degradation path.

## Output for the assurance section

For each critical control produce a row:

| Critical control | Strength | In place? | Effective? | Verified (who / how often / evidence)? | Gap / action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Then summarise: how many critical controls are fully assured, how many have gaps, and the
priority actions to close them. This summary — not the length of the control list — is the real
output of a critical-risk assessment.

## Disclaimer

Control-assurance judgements here are a structured **draft for a competent person to validate**
on the actual site, with the actual plant and people. Assurance status can change daily; a clean
assessment is a snapshot, not a guarantee.
