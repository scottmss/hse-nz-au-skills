# Road transport & logistics safety — good practice (NZ/AU)

Methodology reference for `transport-logistics-specialist`. Grounded in the **AU Heavy Vehicle National
Law (HVNL) / Chain of Responsibility** (NHVR + Master Code) and **NZ HSWA 2015 + Land Transport rules**
(NZTA / Waka Kotahi). Government/regulator guidance is summarised with attribution; the **Load Restraint
Guide** and standards are **verify-pointers** (confirm current edition).

> **Not legal advice.** The HVNL/CoR regime is a **separate body of law** from WHS/HSWA. A competent
> person validates duties, work-time limits and load-restraint methods against the current rules.

---

## 1. Why transport is a supply-chain problem

Road transport is **high-energy, dispersed and time-pressured**, and the people who create the risk are
often **not the driver**: the **scheduler** who sets an impossible run, the **consignor** who demands a
delivery window, the **loader** who packs an unstable load. So the safety model is a **supply-chain**
one — everyone who influences the transport task shares the duty. Australia codifies this as **Chain of
Responsibility**; New Zealand reaches the same place through **HSWA upstream and overlapping duties**.

---

## 2. The regime — Chain of Responsibility & the NZ equivalent

### AU — Chain of Responsibility (CoR) under the HVNL
- Parties **other than the driver** — **consignor/consignee, packer, loader/unloader, operator/prime
  contractor, scheduler, loading manager** and others — each owe the **Primary Duty**: *eliminate or
  minimise the risks of the transport activities you influence or control, so far as is reasonably
  practicable.* It mirrors the WHS primary duty (and CoR sits **alongside** WHS, regulated by the
  **NHVR**, not the WHS regulator).
- The **NHVR "Master Code"** (registered industry Code of Practice) turns the HVNL risk areas —
  **speed, fatigue, mass/dimension/loading, vehicle standards** — into practical controls.
- Breaches carry significant penalties (individual exposure up to imprisonment for serious cases).

### NZ — same effect via HSWA
- No CoR statute, but a **PCBU** that **consigns, packs, loads, schedules or receives** freight owes
  **upstream and overlapping duties** under HSWA, and **a vehicle is a workplace**. Use the HSWA
  overlapping-duties analysis (→ `../../worksafe-nz-specialist/`) to allocate the supply-chain duties.
- Operating rules sit under **Land Transport** law (NZTA/Waka Kotahi): **operator licensing** and
  **work-time & logbooks**.

## 3. Driver fatigue & work-time

- **AU:** **standard hours** under the HVNL; **Basic Fatigue Management (BFM)** and **Advanced Fatigue
  Management (AFM)** accreditation (NHVAS) allow more flexible, risk-managed hours (BFM e.g. up to ~14 h
  work in 24 h); a **work diary** records work/rest.
- **NZ:** **Land Transport work-time rules** — a **break of at least 30 minutes after 5½ hours** of work
  time, a maximum **13-hour cumulative work day**, recorded in a **logbook**.
- **Beyond compliance** — fatigue is a hazard to be **managed**, not just a limit to obey: realistic
  schedules (don't design in speeding or fatigue), **night-work/circadian** effects, rest-quality, and a
  culture where a fatigued driver can **stop without penalty**.

---

## 4. Loading, unloading & load restraint

The **most injury-dense activity** in transport (falls from vehicles, struck-by, load shift) and a core
CoR risk area → flagship `loading-and-load-restraint.md`. Restrain every load to the **performance
standard** (Load Restraint Guide); the **loader/packer owes a duty** for how the load is secured.

---

## 5. Vehicle, journey, speed & driver health

- **Vehicle roadworthiness** — pre-trip/daily checks, planned maintenance, defect reporting; the **right
  vehicle/combination** for the task and mass/dimension limits (overloading is a CoR risk area).
- **Journey/route management** — distances, breaks, conditions (weather, remote roads), communications
  for **lone/remote driving**, and realistic timing.
- **Speed management** — schedules and incentives that don't pressure speeding; speed-limiter compliance.
- **Driver health** — fatigue, **impairment (alcohol/drugs/distraction)**, musculoskeletal load from
  loading/restraint, and the wellbeing of a solo, sedentary, time-pressured worker.

## 7. Critical questions to consider

- Are the **supply-chain duties mapped** — CoR parties (AU) or the HSWA upstream/overlapping duties (NZ)
  — for everyone who influences **speed, fatigue, mass/loading and vehicle standards**?
- Is **fatigue managed** to the work-time/logbook rules **and** as a hazard (realistic schedules, a
  driver can stop)?
- Is every **load restrained to the performance standard**, and **loading/unloading** controlled for
  falls and struck-by? (→ flagship)
- Is the **vehicle roadworthy**, the **journey planned**, and **speed/impairment** controlled?
- Are the **physical hazards routed** — falls, forklift, on-site traffic, HIAB lifting, dangerous goods?
- Could an event be **notifiable** (WHS/HSWA) — and in AU, is it also an **NHVR/HVNL** matter?
