# Loading, unloading & load restraint — good practice (NZ/AU)

Flagship reference for `transport-logistics-specialist`. **Loading and unloading is the most injury-
dense activity in road transport** — falls from vehicles, being struck, and load shift — and an
unrestrained load is lethal on the road and to anyone who opens the curtain/door. Grounded in the **NHVR
Load Restraint Guide** (AU) and NZ load-restraint guidance, within the **Chain of Responsibility / HSWA**
duties. Standards are **verify-pointers**; confirm the current edition.

> **Not legal advice.** In AU the **loader, packer and consignor each owe a CoR duty** for how a load is
> secured; in NZ the equivalent **HSWA upstream duties** apply. Validate methods against the current Load
> Restraint Guide and the actual load/vehicle.

---

## 1. Loading & unloading — the injury-dense activity

The dangerous moments are getting **onto, around and off** the vehicle, and the load **moving**:

- **Falls from height** — climbing on trailers, decks, loads and tankers is a leading injury source.
  **Eliminate the need to get on the load**: ground-level operation, gantries/platforms with edge
  protection, fall-protection systems at loading bays. → `../../working-at-height-specialist/`.
- **Struck-by & crush** — by **forklifts** (separate trucks from people at the dock →
  `../../forklift-specialist/`), by **moving vehicles** (keep the driver in a safe, agreed position;
  prevent **trailer creep/drive-away** with chocks/restraints/traffic-light systems), and by **falling
  freight**.
- **Load shift on opening** — a load that moved in transit can **fall out when the curtain or door is
  opened**; open from a safe position and never assume a load is stable.
- **The dock interface** — dock plates, trailer restraint, segregation of pedestrians and MHE, and
  controlled reversing → `../../mobile-plant-traffic-specialist/`.

## 2. Plan the loading

- **Right vehicle and equipment** for the freight; know the **mass and dimension limits** (overloading
  and bad axle loading are CoR risk areas and affect braking/stability).
- **Load distribution** — weight within limits and **placed for stability** (low centre of gravity,
  balanced over axles, heavy items low and forward of lighter ones); secure against **forward,
  rearward, sideways and vertical** movement.
- **Loading manager / loader competency** — trained people, a safe loading method, and a way to verify
  the load before it leaves.

## 3. Load restraint — to the performance standard

The **Load Restraint Guide** sets the performance standard a restraint system must meet — the load must
stay put under the forces of normal driving (heavy braking, cornering, accelerating, and bouncing):

- **Restraint methods** — **tie-down** (lashings creating friction), **direct restraint** (blocking,
  containing or attaching the load), or a **combination**; choose for the load type.
- **Rated equipment** — lashings, chains, webbing straps, twist-locks and anchor points **rated and in
  good condition**; enough of them, correctly tensioned and angled; protect lashings at sharp edges.
- **Containment** — headboards, gates, curtains (curtains are **not** restraint unless rated), and
  load-rated containers/racks.
- **Check and re-check** — verify restraint **before departure** and **re-check after the load settles**
  (early in the trip); inspect at breaks.

## 4. Specific load types (confirm specialist guidance)

- **Steel, pipe, coil, timber/logs, machinery, containers, liquids (surge), livestock, and loose/bulk**
  loads each have specific restraint methods — follow the Load Restraint Guide's worked examples and any
  commodity-specific guidance. **Logs/timber** → see also `../../forestry-specialist/` for the landing
  end of log-truck loading.

## 5. Critical questions to consider

- Can the task be done **without getting on the vehicle/load** (ground-level, gantry, fall protection)?
- Are **people separated from forklifts and moving vehicles**, and is **trailer creep/drive-away**
  prevented at the dock?
- Is the load **within mass/dimension limits** and **distributed for stability**?
- Is the load **restrained to the performance standard** with **rated, sound equipment**, restrained in
  **all four directions**, and **re-checked after settling**?
- Could the load **fall out when the door/curtain is opened** — is unloading done from a safe position?
- Are the **CoR/HSWA duties** of the loader, packer and consignor met for how this load is secured?

## Sources & discipline

- **AU:** **NHVR Load Restraint Guide** (technical performance standard and worked examples); loading/
  load restraint is a **Chain of Responsibility** risk area (loader, packer, consignor duties); WHS law
  in parallel. **NZ:** NZTA/Waka Kotahi load-restraint guidance and the truck-loading rules, within
  **HSWA** upstream/overlapping duties.
- Falls, forklift and on-site-traffic methods live with their SMEs (links above). Confirm the current
  Load Restraint Guide edition and any commodity-specific guidance before relying on a method.
