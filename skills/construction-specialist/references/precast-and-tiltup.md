# Precast & tilt-up concrete erection — good practice (NZ/AU)

Flagship reference for `construction-specialist`. **Handling, transporting and erecting precast and
tilt-up concrete panels is a high-consequence activity that has killed and seriously injured workers in
both NZ and Australia** — a panel is heavy and **unstable until braced and permanently connected**.
Grounded in WorkSafe NZ's precast guidance and the AU **AS 3850 / AS 3600** + National Code of Practice.
Standards are **copyright — verify-pointers only**. Confirm current editions.

> **Not legal advice; not engineering design.** Panels, lifting inserts and temporary bracing **must be
> designed and certified by a chartered/professional engineer**. This reference is the safety-management
> wrapper around that engineering — it does not replace it.

---

## 1. Why it kills

A precast or tilt-up panel is a **large, heavy, top-heavy element that is only stable once it is braced
and then permanently connected** into the structure. The lethal moments are:

- **During the lift** — insert/clutch or rigging failure, or a panel that cracks because the concrete
  was lifted too early.
- **Before bracing** — a free-standing panel toppling.
- **Brace failure** — an under-designed brace, a bad brace footing, or a brace removed too early.
- **People in the fall zone** — anyone under or beside a panel that drops or topples.

Each has an engineering control **and** a safety-management control — both are needed.

---

## 2. Design & certification (before anything happens on site)

- Panels, **lifting inserts**, and **temporary bracing/propping** are **engineer-designed to AS 3850
  (tilt-up/precast) and AS 3600 (concrete structures)** (or the NZ equivalent good practice under HSWA).
- **Shop/erection drawings** specify insert types/positions, brace positions and loads, brace-footing
  requirements (including required concrete strength at the time of bracing), the **erection sequence**,
  and crane/rigging requirements.
- The engineer provides **signed certification** that is **on site and referenced in the erection
  documentation** before erection starts.

---

## 3. Lifting the panel

- **Cast-in lifting inserts/clutches are preferred** over post-installed anchors; use **rated, matched
  clutches** for the insert type and apply a **high factor of safety** to cast-in lifting components.
- **Verify concrete strength at lift** — panels are only lifted once the concrete has reached the
  **specified lift strength** (commonly **≥ 75% of design strength**, often ~20–32 MPa) — confirmed by
  test, not assumed. Lifting "green" concrete cracks panels and fails inserts.
- **Rig and crane the lift correctly** — load/centre-of-gravity, equalised multi-point rigging, spreader
  beams and the de-rated sling/crane capacity → `../../lifting-rigging-specialist/` (sling angles, load
  share) and `../../crane-specialist/` (the crane, ground, lift plan).
- **Exclusion zone** — no one under or in the fall zone of a suspended panel; tag lines to control it;
  agreed signals; stop in dangerous wind.

---

## 4. Temporary bracing (the control that stops panels falling)

- **Braces are engineered** — number, size, angle and positions per the design; **cast-in brace inserts
  are preferred** over post-installed anchors.
- **Brace footings must be engineered and adequate** — the **footing/slab must have the required concrete
  strength at the time of bracing**, with a **signed brace-footing plan provided by the engineer to the
  erector before erection begins**. A panel braced to an inadequate footing can pull out and topple.
- **Brace every panel as it is set, before the crane releases it** (per the sequence); do not leave
  free-standing panels.
- **Do NOT remove braces** until the panel is **permanently connected and the structure can carry the
  loads**, confirmed and **signed off** — premature brace removal is a classic fatal failure.

---

## 5. Erection sequence, ground & exclusion

- **Follow the engineered erection sequence**; don't improvise the order.
- **Crane set-up and ground** — the crane and any tilt-up "stripping" lift impose heavy, sometimes
  shifting loads; ground/outriggers and the lift plan → `../../crane-specialist/`.
- **Exclusion zones** around the lift, the panel and the braces; only essential, competent people in the
  zone; spotters and clear communication.
- **Wind limits** — set and observe maximum wind speeds for lifting and for free-standing/braced panels.

---

## 6. Transport, storage & handling

- Panels are **transported and stored as designed** (A-frames/stillages, correct support points) so they
  are not overstressed or cracked before erection; lifting/turning ("stripping") follows the design.

---

## 7. Critical questions to consider

- Are the panels, **lifting inserts** and **temporary bracing** **engineer-designed and certified**, with
  the certification and **erection sequence on site**?
- Has the **concrete reached lift strength** (tested), and are the **clutches rated and matched** to the
  inserts?
- Is the **lift rigged and craned correctly** — CoG, equalised rigging, de-rated capacity? →
  `../../lifting-rigging-specialist/` + `../../crane-specialist/`.
- Is each panel **braced before the crane releases it**, to an **engineered footing of adequate
  strength**, per a **signed brace plan**?
- Are **braces left in place until permanent connection is complete and signed off**?
- Are **exclusion zones, wind limits and the erection sequence** enforced, and is this captured in the
  **SWMS/TA**? (precast/tilt-up is HRCW in AU.)
