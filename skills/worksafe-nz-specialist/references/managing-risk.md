# Managing work risks — the four steps (HSWA s 30; GRWM Regs 2016)

WorkSafe NZ's process for managing work health and safety risks, and the legal duty behind it. This
is the **duty/process** view (what the law requires and the order of controls); for building a
structured bow tie and scoring control assurance, hand off to `../../critical-risk-manager/`. **Not
legal advice** — section/regulation numbers are verify-pointers.

## The legal duty

- **Management of risks (HSWA s 30).** A PCBU must **eliminate** risks to health and safety **so far
  as is reasonably practicable**; and if elimination is not reasonably practicable, **minimise** them
  so far as is reasonably practicable.
- **Reasonably practicable (s 22).** What is reasonably able to be done — weighing likelihood and
  severity of harm against what is known about the risk and the availability, suitability and cost of
  controls (cost only where **grossly disproportionate**).
- **Health and Safety at Work (General Risk and Workplace Management) Regulations 2016 (GRWM Regs)**
  set out the **hierarchy/order of control measures** a PCBU must apply when minimising risk.

## The four steps (Plan–Do–Check–Act)

1. **Identify** (Plan) — find the hazards and risks. Think through the work, the plant/substances,
   the people, and how harm could occur. Draw on worker knowledge, incident history, industry
   guidance, safety data sheets, and the experience of other PCBUs you share duties with.
2. **Assess** (Plan) — where needed, understand the risk: how likely, how severe, who is exposed, and
   which are the **critical risks** (potential for death or serious harm). A formal risk assessment
   is not always required, but is valuable where the risk is significant, uncertain, or controlled by
   a few key barriers.
3. **Control** (Do) — apply the hierarchy below: eliminate first; if not reasonably practicable,
   minimise. Implement the controls and make sure people know and use them.
4. **Review** (Check + Act) — monitor that controls are in place and effective, and review them on an
   **ongoing basis** — after an incident, a change to work/plant/people, when a new hazard emerges, or
   when a control is found wanting — then adjust.

## The hierarchy of controls (GRWM Regs — order of control)

Apply in order; do not jump to weak controls because they are cheaper or easier:

1. **Eliminate** the risk so far as is reasonably practicable (s 30) — the only way to remove it
   entirely (e.g. design out the hazard; don't do the hazardous task).

If elimination is not reasonably practicable, **minimise** so far as is reasonably practicable, by
one or more of the following (higher = more reliable, less dependent on human behaviour):

2. **Substitution** — substitute (wholly or partly) the hazard with something of lesser risk.
3. **Isolation** — isolate the hazard from people (physical separation, exclusion, barriers).
4. **Engineering controls** — guards, interlocks, ventilation, rated equipment, fail-safes.

…and if risk remains after the above:

5. **Administrative controls** — procedures, permits, training, competency, signage, supervision,
   job rotation.

…and if risk **still** remains:

6. **Personal protective equipment (PPE)** — the last line; protects one person, depends on correct
   use, fails quietly.

> **Hard controls (1–4) over soft controls (5–6).** A critical (fatal) risk carried only by
> administrative controls + PPE is a red flag — see `../../critical-risk-manager/`.

## Key principles (WorkSafe)

- **Focus on your critical risks first**, before managing less serious risks.
- **Engage workers** — ask for their input when identifying risks and when choosing controls; they
  know how the work is really done. (Worker engagement/participation is itself an HSWA duty.)
- **Review on an ongoing basis** to catch new risks and confirm controls still work.
- **Paperwork supports the process — it is not a substitute** for actual control measures. A risk
  assessment on file does not control anything; the control on the job does.

## How to use this

- Walk a hazard through the four steps and produce: identified risk → assessment (if needed) →
  controls **in hierarchy order** (showing why higher controls were/weren't reasonably practicable) →
  review trigger and frequency.
- When the risk is **critical**, escalate to a full bow tie and control-assurance pass in
  `../../critical-risk-manager/` — this reference frames the *duty and order*; that skill builds the
  *barrier-by-barrier* analysis and scores residual risk.
