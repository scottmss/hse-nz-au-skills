# Overlapping duties (HSWA 2015 s 34)

When businesses work together they will likely **share H&S duties in relation to the same matter** —
these are *overlapping duties*. Based on WorkSafe NZ's *Overlapping Duties* guidance and HSWA 2015.
**Not legal advice**; section numbers are verify-pointers — confirm the current text.

## The duty — the "3 Cs"

**HSWA 2015 s 34:** if more than one PCBU has a duty in relation to the same matter, each PCBU must,
**so far as is reasonably practicable, consult, cooperate with, and coordinate activities** with all
other PCBUs who have a duty in relation to that same matter.

- The point of s 34 is to **close gaps** — to make sure nothing falls between businesses, and that
  effort is not wastefully duplicated.
- Cooperating includes implementing what was agreed in consultation, and **refraining from acting in
  a way that compromises another PCBU's H&S actions**.
- A duty holder cannot contract out of the duty. Failing the s 34 duty is an offence (indicative
  penalties: a fine up to **$20,000** for an individual / up to **$100,000** for any other person such
  as a company — verify current figures).

## When do duties overlap?

Two situations — **a shared workplace is not required**:

1. **Shared workplace** — more than one business controls/influences the work on the same site (e.g.
   a shopping centre, a port, a building site). *Example: a coffee cart operating inside a garden
   centre shares overlapping duties with the garden centre.*
2. **Contracting chain** — contractors and subcontractors providing services to a head contractor or
   client, who **don't necessarily share a workplace**. *Example: an Otago farmer contracts a
   Tauranga logging company to harvest a woodlot near Rotorua; the logger subcontracts another firm to
   harvest and transport. None share a site, but all hold overlapping duties.*

*Shared duty without a shared workplace — worked example:* the duty to **notify WorkSafe** of a death,
notifiable injury/illness, or notifiable incident can overlap. The parties consult and agree the
subcontractor doing the work is **best placed to notify**; that party would then tell the others that
a notification was made.

## Meeting the duty — what good looks like

A business is more likely to meet the s 34 duty if it:

- **Plans ahead** — thinks through every stage of the work and how it could affect other businesses
  and the public;
- **Identifies the H&S risks** that need managing;
- **Consults** the other businesses to **agree how to control each risk**;
- **Consults to decide which business(es) are best placed to control each risk**; and
- **Clearly defines roles, responsibilities and actions**, and explains them so everyone knows what to
  expect.

### Where gaps happen vs the benefits of the 3 Cs

| Gaps can happen when… | Benefits of consult/cooperate/coordinate |
|---|---|
| There's a lack of understanding of how each business's work adds to the risk — in the workplace as a whole or along a chain of activities | Businesses on a shared site or in a chain work together to sort out issues |
| A business **assumes** another is taking care of a particular H&S issue | Everyone is aware of their roles and responsibilities |
| The business managing a risk is **not the one best placed** to do so | Helps work out who is best positioned to manage identified risks |
| Businesses **don't know what other work** is happening, and when | Smoother, more efficient arrangements — which can lead to productivity gains |

## The consultation questions (the 3 Cs in practice)

Get the right people from each business together to exchange information about:

**Work activities**
- What will each business do — how, when, where, and with what plant/substances?
- Which workers will be involved? What other people (not just workers) may be affected?
- How could one business's activities affect another's work — and introduce or increase risks to
  others (including down a contracting chain)?
- How could each business's activities affect the work environment?

**Health and safety risks**
- What do the workers in each business know about the risks of the activities they carry out?
- How will each business and its workers manage (eliminate or minimise) those risks?

**Ongoing communication on H&S**
- How will workers communicate and have input — and what communication types do they prefer?
- What information might one business need to give another (and its workers)?
- How will businesses engage with workers and others (e.g. joint site meetings, shared newsletters)?
- How will emergency procedures be coordinated?
- **Who will notify the regulator if a notifiable event occurs?**
- What further consultation is needed to monitor measures and identify changes (e.g. a new risk)?
- What facilities could be shared (e.g. first aid / sick rooms)?

## Working together — reasonable agreements

Businesses can make **reasonable agreements** to manage risks collectively, reflected in contracts, to
avoid duplication. But:

> **Entering an agreement does not remove your responsibility for your own workers.** You must still
> **monitor the other business** to ensure it follows through on what was agreed.

- A business is responsible not only to those it employs/contracts, but to **people it influences and
  directs, and others at the workplace** — it must support them to meet their duties, and **not pass
  risk on, nor increase risk** through its arrangements.
- *Example:* a cleaning agency contracts its workers into a hotel; it still owes its workers a first-aid
  duty, so it agrees they may use the hotel's facilities — after satisfying itself those facilities are
  adequate.
- *Example:* a construction company and a courier who delivers daily meet to agree safe site access;
  the builder agrees to warn the courier ahead of time and provide alternative safe access — so it is
  **not transferring risk** to the courier.

## Establishing each business's level of responsibility — influence and control

Each business's responsibility differs and depends on its **ability to influence and control** the H&S
matter:

> **The more influence and control a business has over a matter, the more responsibility it is likely
> to have.**

Influence and control comes through:
- **Control over the work activity** — the business controlling the activity is usually best placed to
  control its risks.
- **Control of the workplace** — control over the site, plant and structures gives some influence over
  H&S for work done there by others.
- **Control over workers** — a business has more control over its own workers/contractors than over
  another business's.

The business with the **most influence and control** (and the greatest share of responsibility) is
usually best placed to manage the associated risks; a business with less may fulfil its
responsibilities by **arrangements** with the more-influential business. **Size and financial
resources do not equate to influence and control** — the business with the deepest pockets does not
automatically carry the most responsibility.

**All businesses should:** discuss what work activities are being carried out; agree the degree of
influence and control each has; agree who will manage what and how; and **monitor and check** how
things are going on an ongoing basis.

## WorkSafe's approach

- WorkSafe expects businesses to be able to **explain the steps** they took to consult, cooperate and
  coordinate, and their arrangements to control risks — and may check those arrangements are working.
- A business must control the overlaps and risks **appropriate for it to control**; the nature of that
  control reflects its influence/control and what is reasonably practicable. Arrangements, including
  **monitoring**, must be **sensible and proportionate**.
- Use **sound contractor-management processes**: choosing **competent contractors**, exchanging
  information, planning and monitoring carefully, and undertaking **post-contract review**.
- **A business at the top of a contracting chain should not push risk down the chain.**

### Case study — Council / "Smoother Paths"

A Council contracts Smoother Paths to maintain a walking track in a council park, and convenes a
meeting to establish each party's duties:

- **Smoother Paths** has the most influence and control over the track work → responsible for risks to
  workers and the public from that work (place signs/barriers during the day; leave the site safe at
  day's end).
- **Council** has little control over the work itself, but controls the **surrounding area** (e.g.
  risks from gardeners/mowers working nearby) → manages those risks (warning signs at park entrances;
  ensure the site is not interfered with overnight as part of its security checks).
- **Council, as the client**, controls **timeframes** → responsible for **not setting unrealistic
  timeframes** that could encourage cutting corners.
- Scheduled catch-ups are arranged to manage ongoing risks and any new overlapping duties.

## Key concepts (note)

- **PCBU** — a person conducting a business or undertaking; can be an individual (e.g. a self-employed
  builder) or an organisation. (HSWA s 17.)
- **PCBU vs worker** — a self-employed person who decides how their work is done and controls the
  risks is a **PCBU**; but if they work for another PCBU that decides what they do and how/when, they
  are a **worker** of that PCBU.
- **Reasonably practicable (SFAIRP, s 22)** — ask: (1) how likely is the risk? (2) how severe is the
  harm? (3) what do you know, or ought reasonably to know, about the hazard and ways to control it?
  (4) what controls are available and suitable? (5) finally, is the cost **grossly disproportionate**
  to the risk? Eliminate first; if not reasonably practicable, minimise. **Give workers the chance to
  contribute** before deciding.
