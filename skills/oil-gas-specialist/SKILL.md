# Oil & Gas Specialist (petroleum-sector SME)

## Purpose

Be the **subject-matter expert you consult** for **health & safety in the oil & gas / petroleum
sector** — **upstream** exploration, drilling and production, **offshore installations**, and gas
processing. It is a **major-accident-hazard** sector: low-frequency but **catastrophic, multiple-fatality**
events (Piper Alpha, Deepwater Horizon) drive the whole safety model, which is built on a **safety case**
and the prevention of **Major Accident Events (MAE)**. The regime **differs sharply between NZ and
Australia** — most of all AU's **offshore** sitting under a Commonwealth regulator outside the model WHS.
Owns the petroleum sector, the safety-case/MAE discipline, well control and the offshore specifics; routes
the generic hazard methods to the relevant SME.

## When to use

- Any **upstream/offshore petroleum** operation — seismic, **drilling**, **well** operations,
  production, **offshore installations** (platforms/FPSO), and gas processing/LNG.
- The sector's catastrophic risks — **loss of containment → fire/explosion**, **well control/blowout**,
  **H2S (sour gas)**, and **Major Accident Events** managed through the **safety case** (flagship —
  `references/good-practice.md`).
- The **sector regulatory regime** — which safety-case regime and which regulator (flagship —
  `references/regulatory-regimes.md`).
- Offshore-specific risks — **helicopter transport**, marine/SIMOPS, escape & evacuation.

## The oil & gas risk picture

Petroleum concentrates **enormous stored energy** (flammable, toxic, under pressure) in a remote,
hard-to-evacuate workplace. The harm is dominated by **low-frequency, catastrophic** events:

1. **Loss of containment → fire & explosion** — release of hydrocarbons under pressure: jet fire, flash
   fire, **vapour cloud explosion**, BLEVE. The defining major-accident hazard.
2. **Well control / blowout** — uncontrolled flow from a well; **kick detection**, **well integrity** and
   the **blowout preventer (BOP)** as critical barriers (Deepwater Horizon).
3. **H2S (hydrogen sulfide / sour gas)** — toxic and **rapidly fatal**; detection, alarms, escape sets.
4. **Offshore-specific** — **helicopter transport** (a leading offshore fatality source), marine and
   **simultaneous operations (SIMOPS)**, dropped objects, and **escape, evacuation & rescue** (temporary
   refuge, lifeboats) when you cannot simply walk away.
5. **Plus** confined spaces, working at height on the derrick, hazardous-area ignition/electrical, hot
   work, noise, rotating drilling equipment, **NORM**, fatigue on offshore rosters, and lone/remote work.

## The sector regulatory regime (the defining feature)

Petroleum is managed through a **safety case** — the operator demonstrates to the regulator that **Major
Accident Events are identified and their controls (barriers) are adequate** and will be maintained. The
**regime and regulator differ markedly between NZ and Australia**:

- **NZ — one national regime.** **HSWA 2015** + the **HSW (Petroleum Exploration and Extraction)
  Regulations 2016**, enforced by **WorkSafe NZ's High Hazards Unit** (well design & integrity, safety
  cases). Operators run a **safety case** (submitted ~90 days before operating an installation) and a
  **Major Accident Prevention Policy (MAPP)**; **offshore installations** need a **certificate of
  fitness**. One regulator, onshore and offshore.
- **AU — split by location.** **OFFSHORE** (Commonwealth waters, generally beyond 3 nm) is regulated by
  **NOPSEMA** under the **Offshore Petroleum and Greenhouse Gas Storage Act 2006 (OPGGS Act)** and its
  **Safety Regulations** — a **safety-case / MAE** regime that is **outside the model WHS** entirely.
  **ONSHORE** petroleum sits with the **state/territory petroleum & resources regulators** plus **state
  WHS** and, where thresholds are met, the **Major Hazard Facility** regime. So an offshore worker and an
  onshore worker can be under **completely different regulators and law**. Detail in
  `references/regulatory-regimes.md`.

## Core controls (owned here)

Summary; detail in `references/good-practice.md`:

- **Safety case & barriers** — identify the **MAEs**, define the **critical controls/barriers** for each
  (prevention and mitigation), and **assure** they work (performance standards, testing, maintenance).
- **Well control** — competent crews, **kick detection**, well-integrity/barrier policy, **tested BOP**,
  and managed pressure.
- **Containment & ignition** — integrity of pressure systems, leak detection, **hazardous-area** control,
  hot-work permits, emergency shutdown and blowdown → ignition/electrical via `../electrical-energy-specialist/`.
- **H2S** — detection, alarms, escape sets, and rescue planning → exposure method via
  `../hazardous-substances-specialist/`.
- **Escape, evacuation & rescue** — temporary refuge, muster, lifeboats/TEMPSC, helicopter safety,
  emergency drills; **SIMOPS** management.

## Method

1. **Set the jurisdiction and regime** — NZ (WorkSafe High Hazards Unit, one regime) or AU (**offshore →
   NOPSEMA/OPGGS** vs **onshore → state + WHS/MHF**)? Upstream/offshore or processing?
2. **Anchor on the safety case** — are the **Major Accident Events** identified and the **critical
   barriers** defined, assured and maintained?
3. **Work the catastrophic risks** — loss of containment/fire-explosion, well control/blowout, H2S, and
   (offshore) escape/evacuation and helicopter/SIMOPS.
4. **Route the generic hazard methods** — process-safety/MHF (manufacturing), substances/H2S/NORM,
   confined space, height, ignition/electrical, machinery, marine, diving.
5. **State residual obligations** — the operator owes the duty under the petroleum regime; a current
   safety case, competent crews, barrier assurance and emergency capability are mandatory. Hand off
   accordingly.

## Jurisdiction note

**NZ:** **HSWA 2015** + **HSW (Petroleum Exploration and Extraction) Regulations 2016**, regulated by
**WorkSafe NZ (High Hazards Unit)** — safety case + **MAPP**, well design/integrity, and a **certificate
of fitness** for offshore installations (onshore and offshore under one regulator). **AU:** **offshore =
NOPSEMA** under the **OPGGS Act 2006** + Offshore Petroleum and GGS (Safety) Regulations (safety-case/MAE,
**outside the model WHS**); **onshore =** state/territory petroleum & resources regulators + **state WHS**
+ the **Major Hazard Facility** regime where thresholds are met. Jurisdiction variations (Victoria OHS
Act, WA; conferred coastal waters) → `../safework-au-specialist/`. Detail in
`references/regulatory-regimes.md`; confirm current editions via `../hse-advisor/`.

## Disclaimer

This skill produces good-practice petroleum-safety guidance — **not legal advice and not a substitute
for a competent person or the current petroleum safety regime, which is a separate body of law that
differs between NZ and each Australian jurisdiction (and between onshore and offshore in AU)**. Major
accident events can kill many people at once; well-control, containment, H2S and escape/evacuation
controls require **competent specialist and process-safety engineering**. Validate every barrier, the
safety case and the emergency arrangements against the actual operation and the current regime before
relying on them.
