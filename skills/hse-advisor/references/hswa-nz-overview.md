# HSWA 2015 (NZ) — orchestration overview

A high-level framing of the New Zealand **Health and Safety at Work Act 2015 (HSWA)** for
routing and jurisdiction-setting. This is not a substitute for the Act, its regulations, or
WorkSafe NZ guidance, and it is not legal advice.

> **Section numbers are verify-pointers, not quotes.** They are given so a competent person can
> check the drafting against the actual text. The Act is amended over time — always confirm the
> current wording at the official source (see *Authoritative sources* below) before relying on it.
> Deep statutory detail belongs in the `worksafe-nz-specialist` skill; this overview stays at the
> routing level.

## The core duty holders

- **PCBU — Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking** (meaning: **s 17**). The primary duty
  holder — usually the business entity, not an individual. A PCBU must ensure, **so far as is
  reasonably practicable (SFAIRP)**, the health and safety of workers and others affected by its
  work — the **primary duty of care (s 36)**.
- **Officers** (**s 44**). Directors, partners, board members and others who exercise significant
  influence over management. They owe a **positive, personal due diligence duty** that cannot be
  delegated away. The six due-diligence steps are set out in **s 44(4)** (acquire/update H&S
  knowledge; understand operations and their hazards/risks; ensure appropriate resources and
  processes to eliminate or minimise risk; ensure processes for receiving and acting on incident/
  hazard/risk information; ensure processes for complying with duties; verify the above are in
  place and used).
- **Workers** (**s 45**) — take reasonable care for their own and others' safety; comply with
  reasonable instructions and policies.
- **Other persons at a workplace** (**s 46**) — e.g. visitors; take reasonable care and follow
  instructions.

> The duties in **ss 36–46** are the ones that carry the most serious offences under HSWA.

## Key concepts the specialists rely on

- **Reasonably practicable / SFAIRP (s 22).** Weighs the likelihood and degree of harm against
  what is known about the hazard and the availability, suitability and cost of controls. Cost can
  only be weighed where it is *grossly disproportionate* to the risk.
- **Management of risks (s 30).** Risks must be **eliminated** so far as is reasonably
  practicable; if elimination is not reasonably practicable, **minimised** so far as is reasonably
  practicable. This is the legal backbone of the hierarchy of controls used in
  `critical-risk-manager`.
- **Overlapping duties.** More than one PCBU can owe a duty for the same matter (principal,
  contractor, labour-hire). They must **consult, cooperate and coordinate** (the "3 Cs").
- **Notifiable events.** A **notifiable event (s 25)** is a death, a **notifiable injury or
  illness (s 23)**, or a **notifiable incident (s 24)** arising from work. On a notifiable event
  the PCBU must **preserve the site (s 55)** and **notify WorkSafe as soon as possible (s 56)**,
  and keep records for at least 5 years (**s 57**). Precise tests belong in the
  `worksafe-nz-specialist` reference — route there.

## The regulator

**WorkSafe New Zealand** is the primary work health and safety regulator. Some sectors have
designated agencies (e.g. Maritime NZ, CAA). Enforcement tools include improvement and
prohibition notices, enforceable undertakings, and prosecution.

## Standards

NZ commonly adopts **AS/NZS** joint standards (lifting, working at height, plant, electrical)
plus NZ-specific standards and Approved Codes of Practice / good-practice guidelines published by
WorkSafe. High-risk work competency in NZ is handled differently from the Australian high-risk
work licence (HRWL) system — do **not** assume the AU licence classes apply in NZ.

## Routing cues

PCBU, officer due diligence, SFAIRP, notifiable event, WorkSafe NZ, Approved Code of Practice, or
NZ place names / te reo Māori → treat the matter as **New Zealand** and apply HSWA.
