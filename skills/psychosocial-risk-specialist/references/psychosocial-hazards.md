# Psychosocial hazards — taxonomy, regulation and controls

The hazard taxonomy, the NZ/AU regulatory framing, and control approaches for psychosocial risk.
**Not legal advice and not clinical advice.** Regulation/section numbers are verify-pointers — confirm
the current text in the relevant jurisdiction.

## What a psychosocial hazard is

A **psychosocial hazard** is a hazard that arises from, or relates to, **the design or management of
work, the work environment, plant at a workplace, or workplace interactions or behaviours**, and that
may cause **psychological harm** (and often physical harm too). A **psychosocial risk** is the risk to
health or safety arising from a psychosocial hazard.

Two anchors that shape everything below:
- The hazard is in the **work**, not the worker. Controls target how work is designed and managed.
- Psychosocial hazards **combine and accumulate** — several "moderate" hazards together, or sustained
  exposure over time, can cause serious harm.

## The hazard taxonomy

Sweep all of these when identifying hazards (common across the AU Code and WorkSafe NZ guidance):

| Hazard | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| **High or low job demands** | Sustained work overload or work underload; high pace; emotionally demanding work |
| **Low job control** | Little say over how/when work is done; rigid processes; no autonomy |
| **Poor support** | Inadequate supervisor or peer support; insufficient tools, training or resources |
| **Lack of role clarity** | Unclear or conflicting responsibilities and expectations; frequent changes |
| **Poor organisational change management** | Change that is poorly planned, communicated or consulted on |
| **Low reward and recognition** | Effort not acknowledged; imbalance between effort and reward |
| **Poor organisational justice** | Inconsistent, unfair or non-transparent decisions, rules or procedures |
| **Traumatic events / exposure** | Witnessing or experiencing violence, serious incidents, or distressing material |
| **Remote or isolated work** | Working alone or far from help/support |
| **Poor physical environment** | Hazardous, unpleasant or poor-quality conditions (heat, noise, poor facilities) |
| **Violence and aggression** | Threats or actual violence (e.g. from clients, patients, the public) |
| **Bullying** | Repeated unreasonable behaviour directed at a person/group that creates a risk to health |
| **Harassment (incl. sexual harassment)** | Unwelcome conduct that humiliates, intimidates or harms |
| **Conflict or poor workplace relationships** | Unmanaged interpersonal conflict; poor team dynamics |
| **Job/role insecurity; long or unpredictable hours** | Insecure work; unsociable, long or unpredictable hours; poor work-life interface |

## Australia — the explicit duty (model WHS)

The harmonised model WHS jurisdictions added explicit psychosocial provisions to the **model WHS
Regulations (regs 55A–55D)**:

- **reg 55A** — meaning of **psychosocial hazard**.
- **reg 55B** — meaning of **psychosocial risk**.
- **reg 55C** — duty to **manage psychosocial risks**: eliminate SFAIRP, and if not, **minimise** via
  the **hierarchy of control measures**.
- **reg 55D** — **matters to have regard to** when determining control measures — including the
  duration, frequency and severity of exposure; how hazards may **interact or combine**; the design of
  work, systems of work, management of work and plant; workplace interactions/behaviours; and
  information available about the hazards and risks.

Supported by the **Code of Practice *Managing psychosocial hazards at work*** (Safe Work Australia
model; states/territories adopt their own approved versions). Adopted by the harmonised jurisdictions
(NSW, Qld, SA, Tas, ACT, NT, WA, Cth) — **Victoria** runs its own approach (verify the current
Victorian position). Confirm the **enacted regulation number** in the jurisdiction.

## New Zealand — the general duty (HSWA)

NZ has **no specific psychosocial regulations**. The duty flows from the **general HSWA framework**:

- HSWA's definition of **"health" includes mental health** — so psychosocial risk is squarely within
  the **primary duty of care (s 36)** and the **management-of-risks duty (s 30)**: eliminate SFAIRP,
  otherwise minimise SFAIRP.
- WorkSafe NZ guidance — **Managing psychosocial risks at work** (2024) and **mentally healthy work**
  resources — sets the good-practice expectation: manage psychosocial risk with the **same rigour as
  physical risk**, using a principle-based risk-management approach.

So in NZ the *method* is the same; there is simply no separate regulation — cite HSWA's general duties
and WorkSafe guidance, not AU regulation numbers.

## Identifying and assessing — the signals

Psychosocial hazards are often invisible from the top, so use multiple sources and **consult workers
directly**:

- **Worker consultation and participation** (essential — and itself a duty).
- Surveys and pulse checks (psychosocial climate, demands, support, fairness).
- **Turnover, absence and leave** patterns; **exit interviews**.
- **Complaints, grievances, near-misses**, and reports of conflict/bullying/violence.
- Workload and rostering data; incident and EAP-usage trends (de-identified).

When assessing, weigh **frequency × duration × severity** of exposure, and explicitly consider how
several hazards **combine** for the same group.

## Controlling — at the source, by the hierarchy

Control the **work**, highest-order first; individual supports are secondary:

| Hierarchy | Psychosocial example |
|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Remove the hazard — e.g. redesign a role to remove chronic overload; remove exposure to abusive customers (barriers, policy, refusing service) |
| **Substitute / Isolate / Engineering** | Change *how* work is done — rebalance workload, adjust rosters/pace, rotate exposure to distressing work, physical security against violence, lone-worker systems |
| **Administrative** | Clear roles and expectations; fair and consulted change processes; supportive supervision; reasonable-behaviour/anti-bullying policies and their enforcement; manager capability; debrief/peer-support systems |
| **PPE-equivalent / individual** | Worker-level supports — EAP, counselling, resilience or mental-health-literacy training. **These help people cope; they do not remove the hazard.** Never rely on them as the primary control |

> The most common failing is treating **EAP/resilience training as the control** for what is really a
> **work-design** problem (e.g. chronic understaffing). Fix the work first.

Then **consult on the controls, implement, and review** their effectiveness — work and people change,
so psychosocial controls need re-checking.

## A note on people and privacy

- Frame everything around **work and systems**, not individuals' personalities or perceived
  weakness — that's both more effective and less harmful.
- Handle any **personal information** (health, complaints, survey responses) in line with **privacy**
  obligations; keep individual data confidential and aggregate/de-identify where possible.
- Bullying, harassment, sexual harassment and violence may also engage **employment-law and
  human-rights** regimes — flag for HR/legal input alongside the WHS duty.
