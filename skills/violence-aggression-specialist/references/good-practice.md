# Work-related violence & aggression — good practice & competency

Detailed good-practice controls, governing guidance, qualifications and critical questions for
work-related violence and aggression. Generic and company-agnostic, distilled from WorkSafe NZ
**"Violence at work"** guidance and Safe Work Australia's **work-related violence and aggression**
guidance. **Not legal advice, not security/policing advice, not clinical advice.** Treat references as
verify-pointers.

## Governing guidance

- **NZ:** WorkSafe **"Violence at work"** (and customer-service-areas guidance). Managed under the
  **general HSWA duties** — the definition of *health* includes **mental health**, so threats and
  aggression are squarely in scope.
- **AU:** Safe Work Australia **work-related violence and aggression** guidance under the model WHS
  duties; the **psychosocial** provisions also apply to the psychological-harm dimension.
- No single dedicated **Code** in either country — manage violence as a **critical risk** using the
  general duties and this good practice.

## What it is

**Work-related violence** is any incident where a person is **abused, threatened or assaulted** in
circumstances relating to their work — verbal abuse, intimidation, physical assault, sexual assault, and
**armed offending/robbery**. Harm can be **physical and/or psychological**. **Clients and customers are
the most common source**, and serious claims are rising.

## Who is most exposed

Customer/client-facing roles, **cash/valuables** handling, retail and hospitality, healthcare and
social services, transport, security and enforcement, and especially **lone, remote, in-home and
public-facing** work.

## A plan–do–check–act approach
Assess whether violence is a risk to your workers → put controls in place → **check** they work through
**incident and near-miss reporting** → **act** to improve. Treat it like any other critical risk.

## Critical controls (hierarchy order)

### Eliminate / reduce the opportunity
- **Minimise cash/valuables** (drop safes, cashless, low float), remove known triggers, manage queues
  and waits, and avoid exposing **lone** workers to known high-risk situations.

### Substitute / isolate — design out violence (environment & security)
- **Lighting, sightlines and layout**; **controlled access**; **screens/barriers** at counters; secure
  back-of-house, rooms and exits.
- **Security technology** — **CCTV**, **duress/panic alarms**, monitored alarms, secure cash handling.
- Separate the worker from the threat where practicable.

### Administrative
- A clear **zero-tolerance policy** with visible **signage**.
- **Training** — **de-escalation**, personal safety, and **robbery/incident response** for exposed
  roles; context-specific training for lone workers.
- **Lone-worker procedures** — communication/**check-ins**, **vehicle/personnel tracking**, buddy
  systems, pre-visit risk screening for **in-home** work, and the right to **withdraw** from an unsafe
  situation.
- **Pre-employment screening** for high-risk roles (e.g. declaration of relevant convictions, within
  legal limits).
- **Easy reporting** of incidents, threats and near-misses, with follow-up — confidential channels for
  sensitive matters (don't force violence reports through a generic system).

### Response & recovery
- **Emergency/duress response** that summons help quickly (and involves Police where needed).
- **Post-incident support** — immediate support, counselling/EAP, and time to recover — for those
  harmed **or who witness** an event.
- **Learn** from events (learning teams / investigation) and feed back into the controls.

### PPE / individual
- Generally minimal for violence — controls are environmental, procedural and responsive. Body-worn
  cameras or personal alarms in some contexts.

## Lone & in-home workers (priority)
Communication and **check-ins**, location **tracking**, a reliable way to **summon help** (duress
alarm), **de-escalation** skills, pre-visit screening, and a clear right to leave an unsafe situation.

## Qualifications & competency

- Exposed workers trained in **de-escalation, personal safety and incident/robbery response**.
- Lone workers trained and equipped for their context.
- **Security** systems fit for purpose and operated competently.
- Leaders trained to **take reports seriously and support** affected workers.

## Critical questions to consider

- Are workers (especially **lone/in-home/public-facing**) aware of the **reporting** process, and do
  they feel safe to use it?
- Is there a plan with **additional controls** when someone works **alone**?
- Are there **tasks, places or people** that make workers feel at risk — and what controls them?
- Do workers know **what to do** if they experience or witness violence?
- Do **security** measures (access, CCTV, duress alarms, cash handling) suit the actual risk?
- Is there **post-incident support**, and do we **learn** from events?
- Is the harm primarily **psychological**? → also `../../psychosocial-risk-specialist/`.
