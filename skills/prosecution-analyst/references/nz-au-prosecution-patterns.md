# NZ/AU H&S prosecution patterns and frameworks

A company-agnostic guide to the enforcement and sentencing frameworks, the recurring prosecution
patterns, and the factors courts weigh — for **learning and benchmarking**. **Not legal advice and
not a prediction of any case.** All section numbers, penalty figures and bands are **verify-pointers**
— they are amended over time and differ by jurisdiction; confirm the current position with the
regulator and current case law. Work only from **public** case law — never from any proprietary
prosecutions dataset.

## New Zealand — enforcement & sentencing (HSWA 2015)

### Offence tiers

| Section | Offence | Indicative maximum (verify) |
|---|---|---|
| **s 47** | **Reckless conduct** in respect of a duty exposing an individual to risk of death/serious injury/illness | Individual (non-PCBU): up to **5 yrs imprisonment** and/or **$300k**; officer/individual PCBU: up to **5 yrs** and/or **$600k**; body corporate: **$3M** |
| **s 48** | **Failure to comply** with a duty that **exposes** a person to risk of death/serious injury/illness | **$150k** (individual non-PCBU) / **$300k** (officer/individual PCBU) / **$1.5M** (body corporate) |
| **s 49** | **Failure to comply** with a duty | **$50k** / **$100k** / **$500k** |

### Enforcement tools (before/instead of prosecution)

Improvement notices, prohibition notices, infringement notices, **enforceable undertakings** (a
binding, regulator-accepted commitment as an alternative to prosecution), and prosecution.

### Sentencing — the Stumpmaster four-step approach

*Stumpmaster v WorkSafe New Zealand [2018] NZHC 2020* set the approach for s 48:

1. **Assess reparation** to victims first — emotional harm and consequential loss. (Reparation is
   often substantial and comes before the fine.)
2. **Fix the fine** by reference to guideline **culpability bands**, then adjust for aggravating and
   mitigating factors.
3. **Determine any ancillary orders** (e.g. adverse-publicity, project, or training orders).
4. **Make an overall assessment** of proportionality and appropriateness — including **ability to
   pay**.

### Stumpmaster culpability bands (s 48 fine, verify)

| Band | Starting-point fine |
|---|---|
| Low | up to **$250,000** |
| Medium | **$250,000 – $600,000** |
| High | **$600,000 – $1,000,000** |
| Very high | **$1,000,000+** |

## Australia — enforcement & sentencing (model WHS)

### Offence categories

| Section | Category | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **s 31** | **Category 1** (most serious) | Conduct with **gross negligence or recklessness**, without reasonable excuse, exposing a person to risk of death/serious injury/illness — carries imprisonment and the highest fines |
| **s 32** | **Category 2** | **Failure to comply** with a duty that **exposes** a person to risk of death/serious injury/illness |
| **s 33** | **Category 3** | **Failure to comply** with a duty |

### Industrial manslaughter

A **separate offence in most Australian jurisdictions** (the model Act includes a note enabling
jurisdictions to insert it). It typically targets a duty holder whose **gross negligence causes a
worker's death**, with very high penalties (long imprisonment for individuals; multi-million-dollar
fines for bodies corporate). **Elements, penalties and commencement vary by jurisdiction — verify the
current position.**

### Penalties and orders

- **Maximum penalties vary by jurisdiction and are periodically increased** (the model maxima have
  risen substantially). Always confirm the current figure in the relevant jurisdiction.
- **Enforceable undertakings** — a binding alternative to prosecution (generally **not available for
  Category 1**).
- **Sentencing orders** beyond fines: adverse-publicity orders, WHS project orders, training orders,
  restoration orders, injunctions, and court-ordered WHS undertakings.

## Recurring prosecution patterns (the lessons)

The same systemic failures recur across NZ/AU prosecutions. Use them as a **benchmark checklist** —
"does our control set address this?":

| Pattern | The lesson / benchmark check |
|---|---|
| A **known critical risk** was inadequately controlled | Is each critical risk identified, with assured critical controls? → `../../critical-risk-manager/` |
| A **known, available, reasonably practicable control** was not implemented (or was removed/bypassed) | Are higher-order controls used where reasonably practicable? Is bypass governed? |
| **Inadequate training / competency / supervision** | Are workers competent and supervised for the task? → `../../high-risk-work-specialist/` |
| **Plant/machinery** unguarded, not isolated, or poorly maintained | Guarding, isolation/LOTO, and maintenance regimes in place and verified? |
| **Prior warnings, near-misses or audit findings ignored** | Do hazard/near-miss reports reach decision-makers and get actioned? (foreseeability) |
| **Failure to consult, cooperate and coordinate** across PCBUs | Are overlapping duties managed in contracting chains? → overlapping-duties references |
| **No safe system of work / procedure** for the task | Is there an SOP and a task analysis? → `../../sop-author/`, `../../task-analysis-author/` |

The common thread: the risk was **foreseeable** and a **reasonably practicable control existed**. That
is precisely the SFAIRP test the duty skills apply.

## Factors that aggravate vs mitigate (educational)

Used to motivate prevention — **not** to estimate a number for a real case.

| Aggravating | Mitigating |
|---|---|
| Obvious/foreseeable hazard; large departure from industry standard or accepted practice | Cooperation with the regulator and the investigation |
| Prior warnings, near-misses or notices ignored | Early guilty plea (discount); genuine remorse |
| Vulnerability of the worker(s) | Prompt remediation; reparation/amends paid |
| Profit or cost-saving from the breach | Good prior compliance record |
| Poor prior compliance history | Reasonable steps taken (even if insufficient) |
| Multiple or systemic failures | Financial capacity / ability to pay (Step 4 in NZ) |

## How to learn from a prosecution (method)

1. State the **duty failure** in general terms (which duty, breached how).
2. List the **recurring contributing factors** present (map to the patterns above).
3. Identify the **reasonably practicable control(s)** that would have broken the chain.
4. Convert each into an **actionable check** for your own organisation.
5. **Benchmark** your current controls against those checks and record the gaps.
6. If you cite a real case, keep it **public and factual**, frame it as a **lesson** (not blame), and
   note it is illustrative — outcomes turn on specific facts and current law.
