# Board H&S reporting & critical-risk deep dives

How to design or critique the information a board sees, so it drives **curious inquiry** rather than
false comfort. From the IoD/WorkSafe guide (esp. Appendix 2). The board report is the main artefact
of governance — get it wrong and every conversation downstream is mis-aimed.

## Move off accident-counting

- Traditional reporting centres on **TRIFR** (total recordable injury frequency rate) and **LTIFR**
  (lost-time injury frequency rate). People can escape unhurt from very unsafe work, so the **lack
  of an accident says little about how safe the work was**; research shows such measures can be
  random, non-predictive and unhelpful.
- **Shift focus to the positive contributors** to safety — resources, work design, training, and
  **testing key controls**.
- **Goodhart's Law:** *"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."* Watch for
  metrics that drive gaming or suppress reporting.
- **Benchmark processes, not outcomes.** Differences in risk profile and data collection make
  outcome benchmarking (e.g. lost days) misleading; compare the **quality of processes** instead
  (e.g. the effectiveness of return-to-work programmes).

## Report fundamentals

- Design the report to your **vision and objectives**.
- **Not everything is measurable — include narrative as well as data.**
- Provide insight without overload; avoid trivia.
- Judge **effectiveness of completed items, not simply their delivery** (quality over quantity).
- Examine the **direction of travel**, not just whether targets were hit.
- Incorporate **direct worker feedback** (including verbatim voice).
- **"Fear the green and embrace the red"** — be curiously sceptical when everything looks positive;
  treat dashboards as a **starting point for enquiry, not a decision-making tool**; look for weak
  signals, anomalies and hidden issues; reject "human error" as an explanation.

## The six report topics

1. **Performance** — incidents by *potential* harm; progress against the H&S plan; action
   completion; competence; wellbeing measures (e.g. sick-leave).
2. **Assurance / verification** — assurance findings (number and criticality); completion against
   the assurance programme; actions in response; quality of assurance delivered; emerging issues.
3. **Risks** — focus on **critical risks** and any disproportionate impacts on particular groups;
   critical-control verifications; % fully effective critical controls; variation from procedures;
   residual risk against risk tolerance.
4. **Relationships** — overlapping-PCBU/supplier/partner/client management; worker feedback
   opportunities realised; learning teams held; external stakeholder feedback.
5. **Resources** — turnover/vacancy and succession for H&S-critical roles; contractor proportion;
   preventative vs corrective maintenance; **proportion of overall spend on H&S**.
6. **Due-diligence performance** — officer activity programme completion; leadership learning
   visits; critical-risk deep dives; independent governance-review findings; feedback on the quality
   of officer interactions.

## Example leading / positive indicators

Prefer measures that reflect whether work is set up to go well, e.g.:

- **Incidents by potential** (severity *had it gone wrong* — Insignificant → Catastrophic), not just
  actual-injury counts.
- **% of critical controls assessed fully effective**, trended by critical-risk type.
- **Critical-control verifications completed** and control shortfalls found per "sweep".
- **Variation from procedures**, classified honestly — from *significant variation that increases
  risk* through *no variation* to *significant safety innovation* (so the board sees drift vs
  innovation, not a binary).
- **% of spend dedicated to H&S**; succession cover for H&S-critical roles.
- **Officer due-diligence activity** vs plan (site learning visits, deep dives, awareness sessions).

> Interpret honestly: an **increase in reported high-potential events** after a "just culture" push
> is usually **better reporting, not worse safety** — say so in the narrative rather than reacting
> to the line going up.

## Three-layer control assurance (for critical risks)

The confidence that critical controls actually work should rest on three layers:

1. **Proof testing at the point of use** — by those who manage the risk operationally.
2. **Higher-level periodic reviews** — confirming the proof-testing process works and the inputs
   behind it (training, maintenance routines, and plans for when a control fails or is unavailable)
   are in place.
3. **System audits** — reviewing the whole approach.

Shortfalls at any layer get **appropriately prioritised corrective action**. Resource critical
controls as a **high priority even when the risk's likelihood is low or rare**, because the
consequence is catastrophic.

## Critical-risk deep-dive template

A recurring governance tool — a focused board session on one critical risk. Structure:

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Risk** | The critical risk (e.g. mobile plant / pedestrian interaction). |
| **Description** | What the risk is and the scenario(s) of concern. |
| **People exposed** | Who is exposed, where, how many. |
| **Critical controls** | The controls that provide most of the risk mitigation — listed separately. |
| **Other controls** | Supporting controls. |
| **Gaps / improvements / progress** | Known shortfalls and the actions underway. |
| **Key incidents** | Relevant events/near-misses informing the picture. |
| **Assurance level** | **Current overall assurance level → target for next quarter** (e.g. Medium → High). |

Pair this with `../../critical-risk-manager/references/control-assurance.md` for assessing whether
each critical control is **in place, effective and verified**. The board's job is to *assure* the
control set; building it is `critical-risk-manager`'s job.

## Glossary anchors

- **Critical risk** — the most significant risks for the organisation; typically those with
  potentially **fatal** consequences.
- **Catastrophic risk** — potential for very large consequences (multiple fatalities, or major
  impact over a large area).
- **Weak signals** — subtle signs that may be lost in the noise and tend to become apparent only
  with hindsight; governance should actively look for them.
