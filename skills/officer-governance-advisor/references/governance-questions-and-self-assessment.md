# Governance questions & self-assessment

The questions that turn the framework into practice — because **"the quality of governance is
directly linked to the quality of conversations."** Two uses:

- **Section A — Director questions** to ask the board and the organisation, by principle. Use these
  *in* governance discussions and critical-risk deep dives.
- **Section B — Self-assessment** (group review by the 5 Principles; individual review by the 5Cs)
  to step back and review how well governance is working.

Questions are drawn verbatim from the IoD/WorkSafe NZ guide and self-assessment (2024). Adapt to
context. **Not legal advice.**

---

## Section A — Director questions by principle

### Learn and develop
*Increase officers' knowledge and the board's understanding; learn from incidents by finding out
what happened and how, rather than apportioning blame.*
- Is there a planned programme of health and safety focused learning in place for officers?
- How do we get regular feedback on the practical reality of how work is normally done?
- Are incidents investigated in a way that generates genuine learning and avoids blame?
- Do we clearly understand the PCBU's risks and how it is managing them and other obligations?

### Anticipate and understand
*Anticipate external and changing influences and take them into account when planning.*
- How do organisational change processes consider health and safety at the earliest stage?
- What future health and safety impacts might there be based on our strategy, and how can we
  manage them?
- How do we know what makes work successful, and have we thought about what might interfere with
  that?

### Plan and resource
*Plan for variability; provide the resources workers need for expected and unexpected situations.*
- What direct routes do we have for feedback from workers about how well work is being completed?
- What resources do we have to support critical-risk management and provide sufficient advice and
  support?
- How do we resource workers to help them deal with both expected and unexpected situations?
- How do we manage work that is complex with unpredictable outcomes?

### Trust and verify
*Trust people, but clearly understand the most important controls and routinely verify they work.*
- Do we know what our critical and catastrophic risks are, and the key controls and critical
  systems used to manage them?
- Has an appropriate process been used to identify these controls, and what have we benchmarked
  against?
- How effective are those controls in practice — are they known about, understood, and being
  applied as expected?
- What information is that view of effectiveness based on?
- Are risk controls deployed across all areas where the risk exists, **including in our supply
  chain**?
- Where do we look to find out where work varies from what was intended?

### Monitor and respond
*Monitor what matters in a way that yields genuine insight and does not incentivise unwanted
behaviour; respond constructively.*
- Do our reports give us genuine insight rather than just numbers?
- Are we focusing on significant issues and avoiding trivia?
- How do we react when people report bad news? Does it encourage future reporting?
- **Are we curiously sceptical if our indicators are always green?**

## Section B — Self-assessment review

**Purpose:** let officers and boards assess their own approach against the guide. Two parts: a
**group review** (primarily for boards, based on the principles) and an **individual review** (for
each officer, based on the 5Cs). Either can be used by either, by preference.

**Deliberately no scoring system and no "performance levels."** Such assessments are subjective and
chasing a score adds little. Instead, focus on:

- where **results differ from your preconceptions**;
- obvious areas of **strength or weakness**;
- noticeable **differences of opinion** (between officers, or between officers and management).

Where any of these show up, go to the relevant guidance and develop improvements. A simple
reflection scale is useful for discussion — e.g. **Needs attention · Acceptable · Strength** — but
it is a conversation prompt, not a grade. In the individual review, capture **specific examples**
against each question to keep the self-assessment honest, and consider having someone else assess
you and comparing.

### Group review — extension questions (board, by area)

These *extend* the Section A questions rather than repeat them.

**Foundations**
- How well do we work together as a team — do we balance skills and approach, challenge each other
  constructively, review our own performance regularly, and commission independent evaluation of
  our effectiveness?
- How well do we keep abreast of good practice in governance as well as in technical areas?
- What is our relationship like with the CEO and the management team?
- How robust and comprehensive are our governance processes — do they provide rigour **without
  paperwork overload**?

**Learn and develop**
- Does our formal process for learning about the organisation meet our needs — balancing site
  visits, deep dives, expert advice and formal training — and are we confident we have enough
  knowledge to make informed, effective decisions?
- Are our conversations based on good knowledge of H&S concepts? Do they recognise: how systems
  drive behaviour and decision-making; the need to build capacity to manage unexpected situations;
  and that H&S management systems are imperfect and need workers to make them successful?
- How well do our interactions with workers centre on learning about their context and risks —
  daily pressures and goal conflict, the **reality of work as done rather than as imagined**, where
  work varies and what innovations are needed, and implications for vulnerable worker groups?
- How often do our discussions of incidents focus on **"what happened and how?"** to enable
  systemic learning, rather than **"who and why?"** that may lead to blaming individuals?

**Anticipate and understand**
- Do we explicitly consider what conditions need to be in place to deliver good work under expected
  and unexpected circumstances, and review the resources/training/equipment to ensure they are
  present as consistently as possible?
- When we set strategic objectives, do we consider the potential impact on H&S even when those
  objectives are not obviously H&S related?
- Do we adequately scan ahead and develop scenarios — with plans for those that may have
  significant consequences?
- Do we carefully consider how external and internal changes will affect future work and our
  capacity to deliver it safely?
- Who is in our ecosystem that we share **overlapping duties** with, and how have we considered
  those?

**Plan and resource**
- Do we have multiple, independent routes for feedback to help us plan — and how often do we hear
  from people other than senior management / the H&S manager?
- Do we discuss the complexity of the work environment and plan for complex, dynamic work —
  recognising there is not usually a single right answer, supporting trials/experiments, and
  encouraging diverse views into planning?
- Do we explore whether good feedback loops exist for workers to share how well resources are being
  allocated?
- When management presents plans and resource requests, do we delve into how the requirements were
  identified — who was involved, what assumptions were made, whether they are based on a **verified
  understanding of the capacity required**, and what contingencies/accuracy ranges are included?

**Trust and verify**
- How much effort do we spend ensuring the people who advise us on H&S are reliable and competent —
  e.g. is internal audit carried out by H&S experts or generalist auditors?
- Do the reports and information we get **verify** that our systems and processes are working
  effectively?
- Are verification processes tightly focused on the most important areas and risks — including
  information about **critical risks and their controls** and demonstration of control
  effectiveness — rather than overloading us with minor data?
- Do our verification processes take account of work **actually done**, rather than what is laid out
  in procedures, and show whether variation is innovation to learn from or unnecessary risk to
  manage?

**Monitor and respond**
- Do we, as a group, respond constructively to bad news so people are comfortable delivering it?
- Do we actively seek narrative feedback and insight into hard-to-measure areas — safety climate,
  relationships, psychological safety, effectiveness of our H&S approach, leadership?
- Do we monitor work ourselves through **site learning visits** to understand the complexity and
  real-world issues workers face?
- Do our indicators and reports give genuine insight into the organisation's **capacity to deliver
  safe work**, and can we connect the reported information to the conditions necessary for safe
  work?

### Individual review — the 5Cs (officer self-reflection)

Answer each with **specific examples**.

**Courage** — *be prepared to say you don't have all the answers; challenge and be challenged
constructively.*
- What do I do to create a constructive environment for those bringing us information?
- When was the last time I said "I don't know" or "I don't understand"?
- Do I speak up if I hold a different view to the rest of the group?
- Do I actively encourage people to air different perspectives — advocating for diversity of input,
  particularly from vulnerable groups or those with a different cultural perspective?
- When I am challenged, do I respond constructively and seek to understand, then improve?
- Am I comfortable displaying vulnerability, or is that seen as a sign of weakness?

**Capability** — *continuously improve your own capability; draw on independent experts, partners
and workers as well as management.*
- What personal development in H&S have I had in the last few months? Is that enough? Do I have a
  future development plan?
- Do I genuinely understand enough about what makes good health and safety happen?
- Have I discussed capability/experience with fellow officers to make sure we have the right mix of
  skills and knowledge?
- Who have I listened to outside my immediate governance contacts for greater understanding of
  work?

**Curiosity** — *be genuinely curious about the reality of how work is done and whether systems are
effective.*
- Do I accept the reports presented by management, or do I follow up with curiosity to understand
  more — probing beneath the surface to make sure systems are effective and the culture is
  supportive?
- Do I know what our people really think? How do I hear their authentic voice, especially those who
  may be reluctant to speak up?
- Do I think about H&S implications in relation to core business processes — procurement, competence
  management, asset management, business planning, M&A, incentive schemes, goal setting?
- Do I ask questions I genuinely don't know the answer to, rather than asking to confirm existing
  beliefs?

**Context** — *recognise the context of work, H&S and the broader environment; focus on the highest
risk areas.*
- Do I keep up to date with the external factors in our industry influencing H&S? What's changing?
  How can we influence them?
- Do I understand the day-to-day pressures of our front-line workers and how organisational
  decisions affect them?
- Do I know what may cause internal conflict with H&S outcomes — work planning, priorities,
  resourcing?
- Do I understand the demographics of our workforce and the particular needs of vulnerable groups?
- When did I last step back and consider the broader operating environment?
- Who is in our ecosystem that our PCBU has overlapping duties with, and how have we considered
  those? How have I made sure external voices are invited into H&S discussions?

**Care** — *put people at the centre; the best governance and due-diligence outcome is a healthy and
safe workforce.*
- How do I express my concern for our people in a way that is genuine, culturally appropriate and
  likely to be believed?
- How do I demonstrate that safety must be a core value and **cannot be sacrificed for productivity
  and performance**?
- How is my concern for our people's health and safety, and equitable outcomes for all workers,
  reflected in my actions?
- When responding to events, do our systems put people at the centre of things?
- Do I know how we treat injured workers and get them back to work? Do I know what our people think
  about that process?

> The 5C behaviours should be **visible** but genuine and sincere — not done for perception — and
> officers should remember their messages may be received and interpreted differently from how they
> are intended.
