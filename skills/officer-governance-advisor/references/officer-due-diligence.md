# Officer due diligence (HSWA 2015 s 44)

The legal core of officer governance. This summarises the duty for governance purposes — it is
**not legal advice**, and section numbers are verify-pointers. For a specific legal position,
consult a competent H&S lawyer.

## The duty

Under **HSWA 2015 s 44**, if a PCBU has a duty under the Act, **each officer** of the PCBU must
exercise **due diligence** to ensure the PCBU complies with that duty.

- **Officers** are directors, partners, board members, and others in a position to exercise
  **significant influence over the management** of the business (e.g. a CEO). In formal structures
  officers usually meet as a Board; many smaller organisations have no formal board.
- The **primary duty to keep people safe sits with the PCBU (s 36)**. The officer's duty is
  different: to exercise due diligence that the PCBU is meeting *its* obligations.
- The duty is **personal and individual to each officer** — **not** a collective board
  responsibility and **not** the PCBU's duty. The two duties operate independently: a PCBU failure
  does not automatically mean an officer failed, and **an officer can fail their duty even where the
  PCBU has not** (e.g. where there is no demonstrable governance of H&S occurring).
- It is **not enough to rely on what you are told.** Officers must be proactive and curious — ask
  questions, confirm understanding, and seek to validate information received.

## The six areas of due diligence (s 44(4))

Statutory "reasonable steps" to take, grouped by the IoD/WorkSafe guide into **Enabling**,
**Supporting** and **Assuring**:

| Group | # | Reasonable step (s 44(4)) |
|---|---|---|
| **Enabling** (personal) | 1 | Acquire, and keep up to date, **knowledge of work health and safety matters**. |
| | 2 | Gain an understanding of the **nature of the operations** of the business and generally of the **hazards and risks** associated with them (its risk profile). |
| **Supporting** (organisational) | 3 | Ensure the PCBU has, and uses, **appropriate resources and processes to eliminate or minimise** risks to H&S. |
| | 4 | Ensure the PCBU has **processes for receiving and considering information** about incidents, hazards and risks, and **responds in a timely way** (information gets to the top and is actioned). |
| **Assuring** | 5 | Ensure the PCBU has, and implements, **processes for complying with its duties** under the Act. |
| | 6 | **Verify** that the resources and processes in steps 3–5 are **in place and being used**. |

Due diligence is **not limited** to these six — they are the floor, not the ceiling.

## The "reasonable officer" test

Officers must exercise the **care, diligence and skill that a reasonable officer would exercise in
the same circumstances**, taking into account the nature of the business and the officer's position
and responsibilities. What is reasonable scales with context — more is expected of, say, the chair
of a board H&S committee than of a newly appointed officer in their first weeks.

## Reliance on advice and delegation

- Officers may seek **expert and internal advice** — but the **reliance must be reasonable**, and
  the adviser **demonstrably capable and credible**. Officers still need enough H&S knowledge to
  **ask the right questions** and judge the answers.
- Officers **cannot delegate the obligation** itself, though they can delegate **specific tasks**.

## Exemptions (cannot be prosecuted for the officer duty)

Elected members of local authorities and community boards, school board trustees (other than the
Principal), and **volunteer officers** cannot be prosecuted for failing the officer due-diligence
duty. (Other duties under the Act may still apply.)

## New-officer induction

A new officer should quickly understand: what the organisation does; who the key people are;
**worker demographics including any higher-risk groups**; the other organisations the PCBU works
with or relies on; the key H&S issues and how they are managed; and **their own due-diligence
obligations and the reasonable steps** to fulfil them. Best delivered face-to-face with early site
visits, weighted toward the **critical risks**. (See the Foundations table in
`governance-framework.md`.)

## How the 5 Principles map to the s 44 obligations

The guide maps its governance principles onto the officer obligations (the principles are a way to
*do* due diligence, but do not follow the Act's structure — always check against the Act itself):

| Principle | Officer obligation it serves |
|---|---|
| Learn and develop | Understand H&S concepts and models (knowledge — step 1) |
| Anticipate and understand | Understand the business and its risk profile (step 2) |
| Plan and resource | Ensure access to resources and systems to manage risk (step 3) |
| Trust and verify | Ensure compliance (step 5) **and** verify system performance (step 6) |
| Monitor and respond | Ensure information about hazards/risks/incidents gets to the top and is actioned (step 4), and verify |

## Using this as a coverage check

For a due-diligence review, walk all six areas and, for each, capture: **what the officer actually
does**, the **evidence** for it (records, training, site visits, deep dives, assurance findings),
and the **gap + action**. Keeping records of this is itself part of discharging the duty.
