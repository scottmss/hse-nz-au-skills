# Vehicles, mobile plant & traffic — good practice, standards & competency

Detailed good-practice controls, governing guidance, qualifications and critical questions for
vehicles, mobile plant and workplace traffic. Generic and company-agnostic, distilled from WorkSafe NZ
**Managing work site traffic** good-practice guidelines and Safe Work Australia's workplace
traffic-management guidance. **Not legal advice**; guidance changes — treat references as verify-pointers.

## Governing guidance

- **NZ (workplace/site):** WorkSafe **Managing work site traffic — good practice guidelines**; "Site
  traffic management"; "Vehicles and mobile plant".
- **NZ (on/near a live road):** NZTA's **New Zealand Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (NZGTTM)** —
  a risk-based approach replacing CoPTTM (mandatory for maintenance/capital road projects). Competent
  supervision (e.g. STMS) required.
- **AU:** Safe Work Australia **General Guide for Workplace Traffic Management** and forklift
  traffic-management guidance, under the model WHS Regulations. (Forklift operation = HRWL LF/LO.)

## The harm — and the one big control

Forklifts and mobile plant are among the **leading causes of workplace death**, and **more than half of
forklift fatalities involve a pedestrian**. The most effective control is to **keep people and vehicles
apart**. **Reversing** is a recurring fatal pattern.

## Critical controls (hierarchy order)

### Eliminate the interaction
- Remove the need for the vehicle/plant, or do the task when/where people aren't present (e.g. deliveries
  only outside pedestrian hours).
- **Design out reversing** — one-way systems, drive-through loading, adequate turning circles.

### Separate people from vehicles (the core control)
- **Dedicated pedestrian routes and vehicle routes**, kept apart by **physical barriers** where
  practicable.
- **Exclusion / no-go / plant-free zones** around operating mobile plant; keep pedestrians out of
  loading and manoeuvring areas.
- **Scheduling** so pedestrians and vehicles aren't in the same area at the same time.
- **Controlled crossings** where routes must cross — clear ground markings, lights and signs; vehicles
  typically have right of way unless a pedestrian is already on the crossing; **positive eye contact**
  between pedestrian and driver before crossing.

### Engineering
- **Reversing cameras/sensors**, proximity-detection systems, mirrors.
- **Warning systems** — reversing alarms, beacons/flashing lights, audible warnings; ensure they work.
- Good **lighting**, **speed limits/calming**, firm and level **ground conditions**, sightlines clear
  of blind spots.

### Administrative
- A **traffic management plan (TMP)** mapping vehicle/plant movements, hazards, zones, crossings and
  pedestrian protection.
- **Spotters** where blind spots or close-proximity work exist; agreed **two-way communication and eye
  contact** protocols between plant operators and ground crew.
- **Site induction**, access/key control, and **journey/fatigue management** for driving (plan trips,
  rest stops, weather/route).

### PPE
- **High-visibility** clothing for anyone near vehicle areas; **seatbelts** worn in vehicles/mobile
  plant where fitted.

## Qualifications & competency

- **Drivers/operators** hold the correct **licence + endorsements** and are competent for the
  vehicle/plant and conditions.
- **Forklifts** — NZ: **F endorsement** to operate on a road plus operator competency; AU: **HRWL class
  LF (forklift) / LO (order-picker)**. → `../../high-risk-work-specialist/`.
- **Mobile plant** operators competent for the specific machine.
- **Traffic controllers / spotters** trained; **road/roadside** work supervised per **NZGTTM** (e.g.
  STMS) — confirm the current requirement.

## Critical questions to consider

- Can **people and vehicles be separated** (routes, barriers, zones, scheduling)? Where they must
  meet, is the **crossing** controlled?
- Is **reversing** eliminated where possible; where not, are cameras/sensors, a **spotter** and an
  exclusion zone in place?
- Are there **blind spots, poor visibility, or close-proximity** areas — and what controls them?
- Is there a **traffic management plan**, and is it followed and current?
- Are **operators competent and licensed** (incl. forklift endorsement/HRWL)?
- What **communication/eye-contact** protocol applies between plant operators and ground crew?
- Is the work **on or near a live road** (→ NZGTTM)? Are the **public** protected near the site?
