# SOP structure and good practice

The section-by-section structure of a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) / safe system of work, and
the principles that make one effective. Generic and company-agnostic — if an organisation has its own
template, brand and house style, apply that separately; this reference is the **method**, not a brand.
**Not legal advice.**

## What a good SOP is (principles)

- **Written for the person doing the work** — plain language, operator-level, no unnecessary jargon.
- **Method + safety integrated** — the controls live *inside* the steps, not in a separate annex
  nobody reads.
- **Competency-defining** — it states what a person must understand and be able to do, and records
  sign-off that they can.
- **Current and controlled** — versioned, dated, owned, and reviewed on a cycle. An uncontrolled SOP
  is worse than none, because people trust it.
- **Real** — it must describe **work as actually done** safely, not an idealised version nobody
  follows. Involve the people who do the work; if they can't describe it in the abstract, ask them to
  **walk through the last time they did it** ("what did you do first, then what?") — recent concrete
  memory surfaces the real steps, branches and easy-to-forget bits.

## Section-by-section structure

### 1. Title and document control
- **Title** — the task/topic and that it is a Standard Operating Procedure.
- **Document control block** — scope (who/what it applies to), version number, issue date, author,
  reviewer, owner/approver, and **next review date**.
- **Record of amendments** — version · date · changed by · summary of changes. Keeps the history
  auditable.

### 2. Purpose and scope
- **Purpose** — why the procedure exists and what it achieves.
- **Scope** — what it covers, who it applies to, and explicitly **what it does not** cover.

### 3. Roles and responsibilities
Who does what in the procedure, **by role** (operator, supervisor, spotter, dogger, etc.) — not by
name. Make ownership of each key control clear.

### 4. The procedure (numbered steps/sections)
- A **Prerequisites** checklist before step 1 — the access, permits, competencies and plant checks
  that must be in place to start (e.g. ☐ permit to work issued, ☐ plant pre-start complete).
- The actual method, in sequence, as **numbered sections/steps** (1, 1.1, 1.2, 2 …).
- **Start each step with an action verb** (*Check, Isolate, Confirm, Don, Lift, Inspect*) — one clear
  instruction per step; what to do, in what order, and what "good/complete" looks like.
- Mark **decision points / branches** explicitly: *"If [condition], do X (step 3a); otherwise continue
  to step 4."* — and specify **both** branches so nothing is left to guess.
- Use brief emphasis devices where helpful — e.g. a plain **"What this means for you"** summary, an
  **"Important"** warning for legal/safety-critical points, and **"Key actions"** for the must-dos.
  (Keep them as plain structural devices — no mandated brand colours in this public skill.)

### 4a. Common mistakes & success criteria (reader-facing)
- **Common mistakes** — a short table of *what goes wrong · the impact · how to avoid it*, drawn from
  experience and near-misses. This is often the most-read part of an SOP; it captures the "things I
  wish someone had told me the first time."
- **Success criteria** — the observable checks that confirm the task was done right (e.g. ☐ load
  landed and de-rigged with exclusion zone held; ☐ plant returned to safe state). Make them
  observable, not "the job went well".

### 5. Integrated hazards and controls
- For the task's **in-scope hazards**, weave the **controls** into the relevant step using the
  **hierarchy of controls** (Eliminate → Substitute → Isolate → Engineering → Administrative → PPE).
- For a **critical (potentially fatal) risk**:
  - call it out explicitly (name the hazard in plain language and what could go wrong);
  - list the **critical controls** the worker must apply, sourced from the bow tie
    (`../../critical-risk-manager/`) and/or the organisation's risk register; and
  - require a **dedicated, visible sign-off** for each critical control (not bundled with generic
    competency rows).
- For standard hazards, a brief mention in the relevant section plus a competency sign-off row is
  usually enough.

### 6. Competency sign-off
- A **competency checklist** at the end of each section: the things the worker must understand or be
  able to do, with a column to mark "explained and understood / demonstrated".
- Space for **trainer/supervisor comments**.

### 7. Declaration / final sign-off
- A **trainee/worker declaration** (understands the SOP, will follow it, knows they may stop work if
  not competent to do it safely) and a **trainer/supervisor declaration** (has explained and observed
  the work and considers the person competent), each with signature and date.
- For critical risks, the declaration should reference that the **critical-control** requirements were
  covered and understood.
- Use generic, plain wording — do **not** import any specific organisation's declaration text.

### 8. Review cycle
State when the SOP is reviewed: on change of plant/process/people, after an incident or near-miss, on
an audit finding, and at a defined **maximum interval** (e.g. periodically). Record reviews in the
amendments table.

## Reviewing or updating an existing SOP

- Preserve the **content images/diagrams** that carry meaning (equipment photos, site diagrams) — they
  are often the most useful part; place them in the equivalent new section.
- Refresh the **steps and controls** against current practice and the current risk
  assessment/register.
- Bump the **version**, set a new **issue/review date**, and add an **amendments** row describing what
  changed.
- Re-confirm any **critical-control** sign-offs are still present and correct.

## Quality checks before issuing

- Document control complete (scope, version, dates, author, reviewer, owner, next review).
- Record of amendments present and updated.
- Purpose and scope clear; scope states what's excluded.
- Steps are plain-language, sequential, one instruction each.
- In-scope hazards integrated; **critical risks called out with their controls and a dedicated
  sign-off**.
- Competency checklist per section; declaration/final sign-off included.
- Review cycle stated.
- **Brand-neutral** unless the user supplied their own template.
